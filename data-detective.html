What this taught me

A few lessons baked into this lab

The first plausible answer isn't always the right one

Every case has at least one tempting wrong answer. Real root-cause analysis means ruling things out with evidence, not picking the first story that sounds right.

One anomalous segment beats a scary headline number

"Churn doubled" sounds like a crisis. "Churn doubled in exactly one new cohort" is a completely different, much smaller problem.

Timing is the cheapest clue you'll ever get

In almost every real investigation, lining up "when it changed" against "what else changed that day" solves half the mystery immediately.

Revenue and profit ask different questions

A best-seller by revenue can be a money-loser once returns, costs, and rework are counted — the headline metric and the real answer aren't always the same number.
